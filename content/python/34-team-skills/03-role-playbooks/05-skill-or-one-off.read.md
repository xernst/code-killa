# When a skill is worth it, and when it isn't

A skill is not free. Someone writes it, someone reviews it, someone
keeps it current. That cost is small, but it's real, and it means not
every task should become a skill. Here's how to judge.

## Build a skill when the task is

- **Recurring.** It happens regularly, weekly, or many times a week.
  A skill amortizes its setup cost over many uses. A task done once
  never earns it back.
- **Standardized.** There's a right way to do it, and you want that
  way followed. If the task is genuinely different every time, there's
  no fixed playbook to capture.
- **Done by more than one person.** The payoff of a skill is
  consistency across people. A task only you ever do has less to gain,
  though a skill can still be a useful personal checklist.
- **Costly to get wrong.** The more a sloppy or inconsistent result
  hurts, the more a skill's reliability is worth.

A task that hits most of those should be a skill.

## Just prompt when the task is

- A genuine one-off, or something you do rarely.
- Exploratory: you're thinking, not following a procedure.
- Still changing: you haven't settled on the right way yet, so
  freezing it into a skill would freeze the wrong thing.

## The honest failure mode

The mistake teams make is skill sprawl: building a skill for everything,
ending up with fifty skills, half of them stale, and nobody sure which
is current. A skill you don't maintain is worse than no skill, because
people trust it. Build skills for your real, recurring, standardized
work. Leave the rest as prompts. A small set of well-kept skills beats
a big pile of neglected ones.
