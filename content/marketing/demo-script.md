# Demo script: 15-minute corporate walkthrough (tier 2)

A script for Josh to run a 15-minute live demo for a corporate buyer (HR, L&D,
or a startup CEO). Time markers are a guide, not a cage. The goal: land the
wedge in the first three minutes, show the product is real, then spend the back
half on the accountability layer, because that is what they are buying.

Tone notes: talk like a person, not a vendor. Let the product do the work. No
em dashes when you write follow-ups from this. Never oversell; the demo lands
harder when it is plain.

---

## 0:00 to 0:02 · open on the problem, not the product

Do not open with a feature tour. Open with their reality.

Say, roughly:
> "Your team is already using Claude and Cursor. Every day. Most of them learned
> it by guessing. So here's the question I want to answer in the next fifteen
> minutes: when an ai tool writes your team a hundred lines of code, can they
> tell if it's right? Right now, mostly, the answer is no. That's the gap
> promptdojo closes."

Ask one question and actually wait for the answer:
> "Where in [Company] is that gap costing you the most right now?"

Use their answer to pick which path you demo at 0:05.

## 0:02 to 0:05 · the wedge, shown not told

Open promptdojo.dev. Go to a lesson with a `fix` step.

Say:
> "This is the whole method. ai writes this. it's wrong. The learner reads it,
> finds the bug, fixes it, runs it. Not a video. Not fizzbuzz. The exact skill
> their job needs, 833 times."

Do it live: read the broken code out loud, fix it, hit run. Let the green result
land. Ten seconds of silence is fine here.

## 0:05 to 0:08 · paths, mapped to their team

Open the career paths grid. Pick the path that matches the gap they named at
0:02 (marketer, ops, support, legal, data, and so on).

Say:
> "Ten paths, one per role. Your marketers don't sit through a software
> engineering syllabus. They get the route that ends with them running ai
> campaigns themselves. Each path has a concrete exit outcome, a specific thing
> the person can do when they finish."

Read that path's exit outcome off the page.

## 0:08 to 0:13 · the accountability layer (spend the most time here)

This is what they are buying. Slow down.

Walk the team dashboard:
> "Assign paths by role. See who started, who's moving, who's stuck. Not a list
> of logins. Actual progress."

Then the three things L&D always asks for:
- **Completion certificates.** "Proof someone finished, not just enrolled."
- **Custom quizzes per role.** "Placement and checks tuned to each path, so a
  mixed-experience team isn't bored or lost."
- **Live sessions.** "Scheduled working sessions to keep a cohort moving and
  answer what a course can't."

Say:
> "The thing that kills most training is that nobody finishes. This layer is
> built around the opposite. You will know, with data, whether it worked."

## 0:13 to 0:15 · the model and the ask

Say:
> "promptdojo is free for individuals, forever. Companies fund it. When you
> train your team, you also keep it free for everyone else. Pricing is per seat,
> scoped to your team, and that's a five-minute conversation once you've seen
> enough."

Close with a pilot, not a contract:
> "Here's what I'd suggest: pick one team, run a short pilot, look at the real
> completion data, then decide. Want me to put a one-page pilot outline
> together for [team]?"

Stop talking. Let them answer.

## If you have less than 15 minutes

Cut 0:05 to 0:08 (paths) to ninety seconds and protect the accountability
section. The wedge and the dashboard are the two things that have to land.
